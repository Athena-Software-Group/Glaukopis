Sophia CTI Templates -- v16 (Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct, single-axis TAA specialist
                              trained off the frozen v12 baseline; v15 W1-rev)
================================================================================

This file is the self-contained template manifest for the v16 training run.
v16 is the second specialist branch in the v15 parallel architecture
(v12_plan.txt §3.1 architecture, v15_plan.txt §1 motivation): an
INDEPENDENT narrow-SFT branch off asg-ai/athena-cti-sft-qwen25-14b-v12,
focused exclusively on threat-actor attribution. The v15 W1 experiment
(v12-plus-taa, trained on the v14 four-shard TAA slice) confirmed the
parallel topology preserves non-target axes but failed to move TAA Classic
because the v14 TAA shard was 68.5% Canonical alias-resolution data
(MISP.CANON 35.8% + TAA.CANON 32.7%; see v15_plan.txt §3 and the v15 W1
post-mortem in tmpl_gen/templates/05102026/README.md §1.2). Canonical
alias resolution is NOT measured by the formal TAA Classic benchmark, so
the model spent ~22.4K rows of training capacity on a task the score
ignores while only ~430 rows targeted the JSON-shaped attribution surface
(JS.TAA.{1,2,3}) that CyberSOCEval-TI exercises.

v16 corrects the shard composition by purging Canonical entirely and
bumping the per-template Count: ceiling on every Classic and JSON-shaped
attribution template, plus the IE/NEG hard-negative discrimination
families. The training topology is unchanged from v15 W1: one epoch,
v9-narrow recipe (cutoff 4096, packing on, lr 5e-6, eff_bs 16) on the
v12 base. Output checkpoint is asg-ai/athena-cti-sft-qwen25-14b-v16.

AB.TAA.1 Instruction: You are a CTI attribution analyst at an enterprise Security Operations Center performing threat actor attribution from observed tradecraft. Analyse the following anonymised incident summary, which reports the MITRE ATT&CK techniques and malware family observed during an intrusion, and identify the responsible named intrusion set. Consult the MITRE ATT&CK groups knowledge base, reason about which documented intrusion set the combination of techniques and tooling matches, and then state the adversary name.
Question: <*{grp:intrusion-set.name}*>An adversary has been observed employing MITRE ATT&CK techniques {ap1:grp.uses>attack-pattern.name} ({ap1.mitre_id}), {ap2:grp.uses>attack-pattern.name} ({ap2.mitre_id}), and {ap3:grp.uses>attack-pattern.name} ({ap3.mitre_id}), alongside the malware family {mw:grp.uses>malware.name}.
Answer: The joint use of {ap1.name}, {ap2.name}, and {ap3.name} together with {mw.name} matches the documented tradecraft of {grp.name} ({grp.mitre_id}). Therefore, the adversary is {grp.name}.

Count: 3000

SECTION TAA-IE-NEG -- AB.TAA.IE.* / AB.TAA.NEG.* (insufficient-evidence
                       refusal + paired-candidate hard-negative discrimination)
================================================================================
Source: athena-cti-db-internal
Provenance: tmpl_gen/templates/05082026/Sophia-CTI-Templates-v14.txt §G.1
Bodies imported byte-for-byte from v14; only Count: directives changed.
The IE templates draw {ap}/{tl} from the unbound MITRE ATT&CK
attack-pattern / tool catalogue (no group anchor) so the model learns
to refuse on under-specified evidence. The NEG template anchors a
correct group {grp} and a confounder {rel} from the same intrusion-set
catalogue; Per_primary_grouping: false ensures the (grp, rel) pair
diversifies across rows.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

AB.TAA.IE.1 Instruction: You are a CTI attribution analyst at an enterprise Security Operations Center performing threat actor attribution from observed tradecraft. Analyse the following anonymised incident summary and identify the responsible named intrusion set if and only if the evidence is sufficient. If the techniques observed are common across many documented intrusion sets and the input contains no actor-specific tooling, malware family, infrastructure, or campaign reference, do not guess. The final line of your response must be exactly: Insufficient evidence to attribute.
Question: An adversary was observed employing MITRE ATT&CK techniques {ap1:attack-pattern.mitre_id} ({ap1.name}) and {ap2:attack-pattern.mitre_id} ({ap2.name}). No malware family, custom tooling, infrastructure indicator, or campaign attribution information was recovered.
Answer: The techniques {ap1.name} ({ap1.mitre_id}) and {ap2.name} ({ap2.mitre_id}) are general MITRE ATT&CK techniques documented as used across many intrusion sets, and no group-specific malware, tool, infrastructure, or campaign indicator was observed in this summary. Without an actor-specific signal, the available evidence is not sufficient to single out a named intrusion set.

Answer: Insufficient evidence to attribute.
{force ap1.mitre_id != ap2.mitre_id}
Sample: ap1
Count: 2500

AB.TAA.IE.2 Instruction: You are a CTI attribution analyst at an enterprise Security Operations Center performing threat actor attribution from observed tradecraft. Analyse the following anonymised incident summary and identify the responsible named intrusion set if and only if the evidence is sufficient. A single dual-use tool used by many intrusion sets is not an actor-specific signal. If the input lacks a custom malware family, infrastructure indicator, or campaign attribution, do not guess. The final line of your response must be exactly: Insufficient evidence to attribute.
Question: An adversary was observed employing MITRE ATT&CK technique {ap1:attack-pattern.mitre_id} ({ap1.name}) together with the dual-use tool {tl:tool.name}. No custom malware family, infrastructure indicator, or campaign attribution information was recovered.
Answer: The technique {ap1.name} ({ap1.mitre_id}) is general MITRE ATT&CK tradecraft and {tl.name} is a dual-use tool documented as used by many intrusion sets. Without a custom malware family, infrastructure, or campaign indicator, the available evidence is not sufficient to single out a named intrusion set.

Answer: Insufficient evidence to attribute.
Sample: ap1
Count: 2500

AB.TAA.NEG.1 Instruction: You are a CTI attribution analyst at an enterprise Security Operations Center performing threat actor attribution from observed tradecraft. You are presented with two candidate intrusion sets. Analyse the evidence, identify the single intrusion set whose documented tradecraft matches, and explicitly justify why the other candidate is rejected. The final line of your response must begin with "Therefore, the adversary is " and contain only the correct intrusion-set name.
Question: <*{grp:intrusion-set.name}*><*{rel:intrusion-set.name}*>An adversary was observed employing MITRE ATT&CK techniques {ap1:grp.uses>attack-pattern.name} ({ap1.mitre_id}), {ap2:grp.uses>attack-pattern.name} ({ap2.mitre_id}), alongside the malware family {mw:grp.uses>malware.name}. The two candidate intrusion sets under consideration are {grp.name} and {rel.name}.
Answer: The combination of {ap1.name}, {ap2.name}, and {mw.name} is documented MITRE ATT&CK tradecraft for {grp.name} ({grp.mitre_id}). MITRE ATT&CK does not document {rel.name} as the operator of {mw.name} in this combination. Therefore, the adversary is {grp.name}.
{force grp.mitre_id != rel.mitre_id}
Sample: grp
Count: 2500
Per_primary_grouping: false
